Introduction¶

This project will investigate and analyze a dataset of gun-related background checks from 1998 to 2017 for different types of gun background checks, such as concealed-carry permits, handgun purchases, and long gun purchases provided by the FBI's background check system. This dataset shares the number of each type of background check in a certain month for each of the 50 states.

In this project I will analyze the dataset by answering the following questions:

  • Which states have the highest total gun-related background checks?
  • Which states have the highest gun-related background checks per capita?
  • What socioeconomic factors contribute to a state having a high gun-related background checks per capita?
  • What does the trend of gun-related background checks look like for the 5 states with the highest high gun-related backgrounds per capita?
  • Which of these states has shown the highest average percentage change in gun-related background checks over the time frame available in the dataset?
  • What do the overall trends look like for concealed-carry permits, handguns, and long guns?

I know right away that this dataset is going to need a lot of work. First I notice that there are some fields that can be dropped from the dataset and that some should also be renamed. I will also need to transpose the data, in order to have the states as rows instead of columns to match the gun dataset.

In [151]:
#Transposing the dataframe to have the states as the rows, renaming the index column as state, removing spaces 
#and characters from the column names to make them easier to work with
c = census.T.reset_index()
c = c.rename(columns={'index':'state'})
c.columns = c.columns.str.strip().str.lower().str.replace(' ', '_').str.replace('(', '').str.replace(')', '').str.replace(',','').str.replace('+','').str.replace('-','_').str.replace("'",'')

The datatypes will still need to be changed, in order to be more accurate, but this will be done separately, in order to to keep that code interfering with dataframes that will be created later on to investigate specific socioeconomic factors.

Although the findings in this report are tentative due to the limits of the dataset and information available, overall, it can be seen that gun background checks have increased greatly over the past two decades due to greater gun sales. This trend can be seen across all of the top 5 states for gun background checks per capita, especially in Kentucky. It was found that Kentucky has seen a surge in gun sales in the past two decades and it leads the country in gun background checks per capita and in total gun background checks, even despite its smaller population in comparison to larger states. Concealed-carry permit and handgun background checks have seen a large surge in the past two decades, yet long gun sales have stayed rather level but have seen a drop since 2016 due possibly to politics and the emphasis on handguns and concealed-carry permits, which are mostly associated with self-defense. This is often cited as the reason for gun purchases.

In analyzing which socioeconomic factors contribute the most to states having more gun-related background checks due to greater gun sales, I investigated the relationships between gun background checks per capita and education, poverty, median household income, and the percentage of people over 65 and under 18. The three factors that showed a decent correlation to higher gun background checks were education (the percentage of persons with a college education), the percentage of persons living in poverty, and the median household income, which of course was very similar to the poverty percentage factor. The strongest among them all, as can be seen in the dataframe above, is education, which has a relatively strong negative correlation to gun background checks. This, although only tentatively due to the amount of data and information available, that states with a percentage of college educated people tend to have lower gun background checks per capita. Impoverished states contrarily tend to show a higher number of gun background checks and the percentage of people over the age of 65 and the percentage of people under the age of 18 don't seem to have much correlation. While gun sales and gun laws are always a part of political debate, gun sales are still ever-increasing, although a drop can be seen to some degree around 2016. Possibly, sales are beginning to level off or even decrease. With another election coming up in 2020, there could although be another change to this trend very soon, whether that is a drop or increase depends on many factors.
